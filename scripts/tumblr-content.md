### First time away

I haven’t left the country in nearly 20 years. The first and only time I did I was 7, so it doesn’t really count. Now here I am, sitting in the international departure lounge assessing what exactly went right (or wrong) for me to be where I am right now. This quarter-life crisis must have been building for a while now. I can’t think of anyone else I’ve met in my life who packed up everything they had to head into the mountains of China to study martial arts under the tutelage of a Shaolin monk. It’s kinda funny actually that I’m not really blown away by these turn of events. My thoughts have always been like a runaway train. Powerful, unstoppable, but with no one in control. Let’s see where this train ends up. ;-)

### And behind door number 2...

Singapore airport is nothing impressive. In fact, it feels like every other major airport I’ve been to. Except cleaner. It’s the little things that excite me. Like all the prices are in Singapore dollars, but you can pay with Australian dollars, and they’ll convert in and give you Singapore dollars for change. Wow! (Note: Ok, I will admit I’m probably a little too excited about this. But I googled for about 20 minutes to check and see how it all worked, and then, it just worked!!)

My first “culture shock” was when I went to the bathroom. I opened the stall door expecting my porcelain friend and what do I find but a hole in the ground and a bum cleaning hose. No effing way am I ready for this shit yet!! Behind door number 2? My old friend. 

It really is the little things that are going to make this trip a bit of a roller coaster.

### Seat belts optional

After a good night’s sleep, I felt rested enough to try and take on the non-English speaking world. I regrouped mentally, ensured I didn’t leave anything behind in my incredibly tiny room, and full of confidence I stepped outside to get the shuttle bus to the airport. Big mistake. It took a fraction of a second for my boy bits to become girl bits… Even through thermals. This promised to be a long day. 

All in all it wasn’t too bad. I’m now at the martial arts academy in Yantai. The school translator made things easy enough for me, although she is pretty chatty. I don’t mind her questions, but I’m tired, hungry, and for the life of me I can’t think of how to describe Brisbane except by stating the population and that it’s pretty warm! 

Words can’t really describe what I saw on the way here. I’ll post photos for that. 

The traffic on the other hand… Apparently there is a saying that everyone in China is born with a steering wheel in one hand, and a beer in the other. I’m not sure if that is true or not, but I believe it. Road rules are optional (read: non-existent). If you want to change lanes, either fully or just use some of the space from that lane, go ahead. No indicating necessary. Turning across oncoming traffic? Feel free, oncoming traffic will just slow down, wait, or beep. It’s the scariest driving I’ve ever experienced. But, it works. Oh, and I’ve been told one of the official bus routes in Beijing has an illegal turn in it, and goes the wrong way up a one way ramp. Nice.

### Battle damaged

My first full day of training was much more intense than I expected.

I had “filled up” on a breakfast of hard boiled eggs, sweet dough, stale bread rolls and a milky water mixed with milk powder. Breakfast of Chinese champions I’m guessing. The snow was too thick for us to run outside, so we ended up running around inside the training hall for 10-15 minutes and supplemented it with stretches. This was then followed by a pretty full on follow-the-leader stretch session, before we started going through kicks and forms. As I had no idea what anyone was doing, I was allocated a senior student to train me and promptly sent outside. In the snow. At minus 5. It’s amazing how much you learn when getting it right is the only thing that stands between you and the warmth inside the training hall. Unfortunately I didn’t pick it up fast enough, and morning lessons were completed with me still standing outside. 

As it’s Thursday I had the opportunity to attend an acupuncture class. The other option was back outside to train. Alone. Acupuncture suddenly became the one thing I have always wanted to study and I quickly headed down to class. It was interesting. I’d like to read studies that show how effective it really is though. The idea of putting a needle in the side of my nose to improve my hearing or easing the pain of a toothache sounds too good to be true. We’ll see how things develop. 

After lunch and an awesome hour and a half-ish nap, it was back down to the training hall for more laps and stretching. Sure enough, after we were limber and warm, it was back outside. Several of us nearly went down with heatstroke as the mercury had risen to an incredibly warm minus 4, but luckily the power training the Sifu had in mind kept us conscious. Hill sprints x 3. Frog jumps (deep squat, jump forward) up the same hill x 3, duck walk (deep squat and walk) x 3. Relax. But not for long. Now, find someone roughly the same weight, let them sit on your shoulders, and walk up hill, down hill, down to the training hall, around and back to the start. Relax. But not for long. This type of thing went on for about 45 minutes. It was more than enough to tempt lunch to rear its ugly head, but I managed to keep mine down. We then headed inside the training hall to do kicking drills. Now I’ve always thought I was pretty good when it came to basic martial arts kicks, and fit enough to handle whatever drills this masochistic monk had in mind. But I was wrong. On both accounts. High knees, twist your hips, kick. Again. Again. Again. Fucking again. When it was obvious that I did not possess the required flexibility to do what was required well, my Sifu took me to the stretching bars, put me in a difficult position so I could really feel the stretch where needed, and then forced my hips forward well beyond the “comfortable” barrier. I cried. He laughed. It was somewhat similar to a Greek tragedy. Sifu then seemed to like the idea of me becoming his project. I had the power, I just had to be shaped into a little ball of Kung Fu goodness. An hour an a half with that man was way too much. I hobbled out of the hall on not one, but two bad knees. 

And unfortunately, I loved every minute of it.

### Go. Harder. Faster.

### Blood, sweat and tears. And blood. Lots of blood.

Our second training session on Friday was dedicated to those of us who wished to participate in a sparring session with fellow classmates. Whilst we all learn different styles of Kung Fu, those participating in sparring would be competing using Sanda techniques. As far as I could see it was kick-boxing with a few take-downs included. The only strikes not allowed were elbows and knees. Each match consisted of two 3-minute bouts, separated by another pair of fighters. This allowed rolling fights and breaks for competitors.

The first match seemed pretty even at first, however one of the guys appeared to be struggling in the cardio department after the first minute or so. It’s really easy to sit back and watch and make judgements about what a competitor should and shouldn’t be doing, however I really felt for the guys in the ring. They were trying to deal with so many different stimuli. They had the noise from the crowd, they had their Sifu giving them advice, they had a competitor trying to punch and kick the crap out of them and they had their own fatigue to deal with. Once a punch or two get through your defences and you need a second or so to get your bearings, things must really start becoming confusing as these punches and kicks keep coming. And then the blood starts flowing.

Of the three fights that took place on Friday, two of the guys ended up with blood noses and cut lips. It was probably a little unfair that the fights were really unevenly matched in terms of skill, but as our Sifu pointed out, how else will you learn? Something that confused me though was we all learn different styles, Wing Chun, Mantis Boxing, Shaolin, Bagua, etc, but the sparring is all Sanda style stuff. It makes it hard to fight when we’re forced to utilise a style we’re not familiar with and don’t train in. At the end of the day though, a lot of the principles regarding generating power and speed based on your movements and footwork are all relevant. The most obvious of all lessons that we should have been paying attention to would be the idea of moving during a fight. It’s ok to throw a punch or two, or block a couple from one spot, but if you stay grounded there, it makes it so much easier for your opponent to bust your nose.

I’m sure I’m really not ready to go into the ring just yet.

### Time to get serious

I’ve now been training in China for a month and I’ve come to the realisation that if I hope to become truly exceptional at the martial arts, then I really need to step up my training and approach. 

I’ve been watching some of the guys who have been here for a year, and they’re absolutely shabby at what they do. They don’t understand the centerline principle. They don’t understand the purpose of maintaining contact with their opponent. They don’t see how to shift an opponents bodyweight or footwork to their advantage. They’re not fit or flexible.

Then I look at the guys who have been here for 3-4 months and have taken everything on board. They listen and apply the techniques. They train hard. They don’t take days off. And they’re incredibly flexible. I honestly believe the flexibility makes a world of difference for how they perform. This is especially noticeable in their range when it comes to kicking attacks. 

As much as I suffer during power stretching, I know i need to start doing more sessions. One session a week is helping, but I really want to excel.

### Well deserved break

I’ve been pretty quiet lately as I have spent the last week or so back in Australia. No martial arts, no masters hitting me or yelling “more power”, no stair runs, no injuries. The strange thing about my trip home is how many people hang on my every word about what I’m doing. People listen intently, then ask questions as fast as possible to get as much information as they can to paint a mental picture of what I’m doing. They all say the same thing, “that’s incredible, I wish I was younger, I wish I was doing that.” I almost feel like a celebrity now that I’m home. But the weird thing is when I go back to China next week, I’ll be surrounded by people doing the same thing as myself. I’ll stop being so interesting and go back to just being another student.

I’m honestly not sure which life I appreciate more.

## [Sat, 23 Mar 2013 14:30:00] (regular)

**URL:** https://beaux-beaux.tumblr.com/post/46058346325/no-sleep-required

### No sleep required

So much has happened since I left Brisbane last Sunday night. I’ll try to update you all and try not to miss out on any of the exciting details. As unreal as some of this story may sound,  I assure you it’s all true. 

Let’s start with my journey from Brisbane back to China. I got the 2330 flight from Brisbane to Kuala Lumpur with Malaysia Airlines. It was almost half the price of flying with Singapore Airlines so I was happy… Until I had to sit around in Kuala Lumpur for 12 hours waiting for my connecting flight to Beijing. Luckily I had attained Gold Frequent Flyer status with Qantas so I was able to use the Malaysia Airlines business lounge while I waited. It was nice enough, but 12 hours of sitting around is 12 hours of sitting around, no matter how nice the unlimited complimentary tubs of Dutch ice-cream.

1800 rolled around and it was time for me to add an additional 6-7 hours of sitting to my already well worn backside. At least at the end of this flight I’d be back in China and one step closer to a return to training. I landed in Beijing and got through customs just after 0130. I was tired as I had only really slept about an hour since leaving Australia, so I was on the hunt for a bed… Or more correctly, because I’m so cheap, a chair or inviting patch of ground. I walked around Beijing International for about half an hour before I found a spot that others had clearly identified as the “go to” spot for Scrooges like me. Amazingly there were two seats free, so I quickly claimed them and tried to get in a few hours sleep. It took me a few moments to realise that I’m clearly a 4-5 chair kinda guy and 2 chairs was going to make this a very uncomfortable night. I would also have problems sleeping as the person who was sleeping towards my head was obviously a 4-5 chair kinda guy too, but had unfortunately been limited to 3. Oh well, I’ve been doing Kung Fu in China for 3 months now,  I’m used to being kicked in the head. Eventually I realised though why I was so lucky to get these chairs when sleeping real estate was in such high demand. Someone had spilled rum or bourbon under the chairs, so all I could smell was alcohol the entire night. This intense smell combined with old mate needing more leg room meant I was only able to get 2 hours sleep and a really sore neck. I was on a roll. 

The next day (Tuesday) the 0800 flight to Yantai was going to take me one step closer to being back at the Academy and all my friends, so I was excited to get moving. I grabbed a quick coffee in an attempt to keep awake and then boarded my flight. I was supposed to arrive in Yantai at 0935. This would give me sufficient time to buy a train ticket so I could join the others who would be making the journey to Shaolin Village that afternoon. Of course, my journey had been nothing but terrible so far, so why should things change now? After countless apologies and delays the air hostess informed us we had finally been cleared for takeoff… At 0940. Shit.

I arrived in Yantai at about 1040ish to be greeted by a pre-arranged taxi driver who would be taking me back to the Academy. Unfortunately there was no translator so I either had to learn conversational Mandarin really quickly, or hope Mr Taxi didn’t want to talk. It seemed I needed to start brushing up on my Mandarin because Mr Taxi was a chatty bugger. He offered me a cigarette several times, and each time I politely refused. Eventually we arrived out the front of a small building in Muping and Mr Taxi had finally caught on that I didn’t speak Mandarin so he simply said “choo choo”. I just took a stab in the dark and assumed that this is where we would be purchasing my train ticket. Turns out I do understand a little Mandarin. I let Mr Taxi do all the talking as I honestly had no idea where I was purchasing a ticket for. As we hopped back in the taxi he laughed and said something, “surprisingly” still in Mandarin. With my limited vocabulary I understood that there were no beds left on the train, meaning I would be sitting upright for 19 hours… I hoped I was wrong. We got back to the taxi and he started kicking off again about fried eggs or some such rubbish. He offered me a cigarette again, I accepted, and Mr Taxi didn’t speak to me again for 45 minutes. I should have just caved to peer pressure at the start. 

I arrived back at the Academy at 1210 to be greeted by a translator telling me the group heading to Shaolin Village would be leaving soon… Like, 20 minutes soon. I rushed up to my room to pack everything up,  only to come to the sad realisation that I had a whole lot of bedroom, and not much luggage space. I crammed as much gear into my bags as possible, and was forced to leave the rest behind for the vultures to pick at. I rushed downstairs and said my hellos and goodbyes all at once. I hadn’t seen my friends in almost 2 weeks and so much had happened, including birthdays. I felt terrible rushing in and out like that, but I didn’t have much choice if I wanted to get started on the next chapter of my Kung Fu journey. Our small group of 11 wished our friends, translators and masters goodbye and jumped into the taxis. We were all buzzing with energy knowing that we were headed towards the birthplace of Shaolin. Oh, and I was back in a cab with Mr Taxi. Worst luck ever. 

After 3 hours of having every person in China snap our photos like we were some sort of rockstars (the fact that I had a pink rabbit attached to my suitcase didn’t help), and having several weapons confiscated, we were finally boarding the train. I headed to my cabin and found that I would be spending the next 19 hours sitting upright, shoulder to shoulder with two other people. Of course there were only Mandarin speakers for company, so I figured this would be a great opportunity to catch up on sleep. I was wrong. Every 15 minutes a cart comes past trying to sell food, toothpaste, souvenirs, or some other crap. The person selling stuff tended to announce their presence at the top of their lungs. Good on them. The ticket lady would do the same. I quickly retreated to the restaurant cart to be entertained by my friends with card games, magic tricks and beers. Lots of beers. Not to sound like an alcoholic (note: mum, I am not an alcoholic) but if I was going to try and sleep upright with half the space I’d get in a plane, I needed all the help I could get. After being caught with beers by our master and being threatened with a staffing, we headed back to the sleeper cart to watch (and sing along to) The Lion King. Eventually Shifu had had enough of us and sent me back to my cabin. This is where my real journey began. I tried every possible sleeping position I could think of, and believe me, after 17 Chinese beers I was getting pretty creative. Long story short my 19 hour train ride to Zheng Zhou ended with me only managing 30 minutes of sleep. So by Wednesday morning I was operating on a grand total of 3 ½ hours sleep. Awesome. 

My attempt to make it around the world in 80 days didn’t finish in Zheng Zhou. We were yet to enjoy a scenic 3 hour bus ride to Dengfeng. And this is where I managed to get to sleep… For an hour.

We arrived in Dengfeng and were allowed to get out and look around, get something to eat, and stretch our legs. I’ll upload some photos shortly because it’s really hard to describe the atmosphere and food and do it justice. If only the trip ended there. We still had a 30 minute bus ride to go to get to our new home. 

The arrival at the entrance to the Shaolin Village was incredible. The temple itself has become a massive tourist attraction so their were thousands of tourists everywhere. We worked our way through the crowd and caught yet another bus up to where we would be staying. Finally we were home. This is where I would really begin my Kung Fu journey. Only a few hundred metres from the original Shaolin Temple. This really is the birthplace of Shaolin.

### Death in the family

So, today I watched a man die. I’m not sure how I feel about it yet, and I haven’t been to sleep so there are no nightmares to reflect on, but I need to get the events off of my chest.

Prince Ocean (that’s another story entirely, stay tuned or feel free to ask if you want to know more) came to me just before lunch and explained that he saw that a man had collapsed in the alley. I thought nothing of this as A) Prince Ocean is 7, and B) Surely if a man had collapsed, someone would have helped him. I went down to lunch only to have my roommate Ryan run in and say that a man had collapsed at Tyler, one of the Shaolin students, was performing CPR. I figured this was an over exaggeration and grabbed my phone to take some photos of what I figured would be a situation where a man was sitting up drinking a cup of tea thanking Tyler for picking him up from his fall. I only wish that were the case. I arrived to find Tyler and Joe performing CPR on a man that I recognised. It turns out that he was the father of the girl who runs the corner shop where Sebastian and I buy ice-cream several times a day (note: I love ice-cream, don’t judge me). For this story, I will explain that we have nicknamed the corner store girl “Happy Smeli” because she owns a jumper that I’m sure is meant to say “Happy Smile” but the English was poorly translated. Anyway… By the time I got to the scene, Tyler was working hard to do his best at keep Mr Happy Smeli’s heart going. It seemed that no one in China knew what CPR was. Prince Ocean came out to see what all the excitement was about and I quickly lead him back inside to his mum, and then headed back to see what I could do. An ambulance was on the way, so it seemed all we could do was continue the CPR and hope they would arrive with a defibrillator soon. At one stage, Mr Smeli spat blood while Tyler was giving him mouth-to-mouth, so he swapped out with another student, Gabe, and continued about trying to keep things going. By this stage Mrs Smeli was holding her husband’s hand and saying something. It was in that moment that I realised, it didn’t matter what language you spoke, this stuff is universal. Happy Smeli was on the phone crying, pleading… Mrs Smeli was saying something to her husband… Whether you understood the words or not was irrelevant, you knew they were scared. You knew they just wanted Mr Smeli to start breathing I again…

Finally the ambulance arrived. I was so relieved. We all know how this goes. The ambo’s get out, pull out the defibrillator and save the day. If only… Instead… A female ambulance officer got out of the van, looked at Mr Smeli, and headed back to the van to get a wrist mounted pulse monitor. They moved the guys who were performing CPR out of the way, and then did nothing! I apologise in advance if this blog starts getting a little aggressive and I use inappropriate language… But… Now no one was performing CPR, and this female officer put a pulse monitor on Mr Smeli’s wrist. Mr Smeli was blue! His hands. His lips. Cyanosis had set in and this female officer was casual as. A male ambulance officer knelt down next to Mr Smeli, no sense of urgency whatsoever, and tried to check for a pulse in his neck. He said something to the female officer who went back to the van to get a portable ECG and a stethoscope. She started hooking up the ECG while the male officer started using the stethoscope to listen for a heartbeat.

Note to my mum and any future employers, please stop reading here.

But… ARE YOU FUCKING SERIOUS?!?! 

It had been approximately 3-4 minutes with an ambulance on the scene and they had tried to take his pulse 4 different ways, and no retard had even considered pulling out a defibrillator. I was furious. The next thing I know the ambulance officers said something, and then packed up and left. Bystanders then helped carry Mr Smeli’s body back to his house so the family could say their goodbyes.

Like I said, I’m still not sure how I feel about this whole situation, but one thing is for sure, I’m angry. The way the situation was handled by the locals and the ambulance service… It’s insane. I understand that I’m in China and things are different to Australia. But it almost feels like no one gave a fuck. The ambulance officers were meant to be heroes. They were meant to come and save the day, or at least look like they were trying. Maybe this anger is misplaced. I realise that I’m a visitor to this country. But to watch this unfold before my eyes just left me feeling like someone should be made to pay for this negligence.

At the end of the day, that’s not going to bring Mr Smeli back.

Tyler, Gabe and Joe did an amazing job in a very difficult situation. These guys are the real heroes of the day. Even though the outcome isn’t what they would have wanted, they stepped up and that is the most important thing. 

Your actions speak volumes about your character and who you are as people. I know it’s not much, but well done guys.

### That fighting spirit

As some of you may be aware, Friday is mountain run day. Thousands of stairs challenge even the strongest of legs and every Friday our Kung Fu groups need to run up and down no less then the times. Yesterday I thought it would be fun to help one of our slower students up the mountain in an effort to challenge him. I’m quite capable of finishing this run in a top two position, week in, week out. The unfortunate soul I chose as my pet project is a solid last place. Every week. Every. Single. Week.

It started off friendly. A bit of a jog. A little outside of his comfort zone. Quickly this enthusiasm is as appreciated as a kick in the boy bits because it is impossible to maintain that sort of pace for the entire hour plus it takes to conquer the mountain. I didn’t care. I wasn’t being challenged so I was quite happy to skip and dance up the stairs (true story) in an effort to encourage my partner. At the top of the first climb my friend collapsed saying that his lung had caved in and he was unable to breathe. He needed to catch his breath and a solid 45 minute sit down would be the only was he could achieve that. I gave him 4 1/2 seconds before I had him on his feet and jogging back down the mountain. Now, anyone familiar with stair runs will know that as soon as you start descending stairs after a big run up, your legs just feel like jelly. At this stage I didn’t really suffer from jelly legs. My partner however looked like he was made of rubber from the waist down. He was not the slightest bit happy with me.

I made a deal with my rubber-legged friend. If he were to finish in the top 5, he wouldn’t have to do push-ups. Should he slack off and finish behind 5th place, he would be punished with 150 push-ups. And I would personally ensure he did every single one. Unfortunately for him, he agreed to this deal not realising that that meant I would push him incredibly hard to ensure he didn’t have to do punishment push-ups.

### A Traveler's Tattoo

We all know someone who has a tattoo, or two, or in my case, 27. My younger sister has more ink than most Hells Angels and for a number of years I myself have considered getting “inked”. Like most people though, I was a little nervous about getting my first tattoo. So nervous in fact that it took me nearly a decade to decide what to get and where to get it.

Back? Maybe, but I wouldn’t be able to see it.

Leg? Nah, it’d make shorts look… Odd.

Bum? Hmmmm…. Mum will never see it. Bonus!

Side? Are you kidding? Do you know how painful that is?

Penis? … What? Why? Seriously? I’d rather use my testicles as a doorstop.

So after much deliberation I decided to not think about it at all and simply make a spur of the moment decision when I got to the parlour. Classic.

Now, those of you reading this are surely thinking “wait, I know how this plays out”. You expect that that I’d walk in, make some comment about “not being sure” or “what looks good” and then walking out with a tattoo of a pink spotted dragon drinking tea with the Mad Hatter whilst riding a unicycle with the words “Hamsters 4eva” spelled incorrectly somewhere around my inner thigh region.

Some twisted part of me would like to say “you’re right!” and then upload the pictures of my colourful new inner thigh (sans visible testicles of course). However, that’s not the way this story goes. It turned out that I had, in fact, put quite a bit of thought into my tattoo. I wanted the words “de mortuis nil nisi bonum” across my chest. These words are a quote from the P. C. Wren novel, my namesake, “Beaux Geste”.

Let’s run through this quickly. I know what I want and where I want to get it. It seems like all I have to do now is take off my pants and get inked. I know I don’t need to take off my pants to get a tattoo on my chest, but I just find it more relaxing.

Wait! Like the Star Wars saga, I’ve left a crucial plot point out of the start of my story. What I strategically forgot to mention is that I planned to get my first tattoo in Thailand.

Yes, Thailand.

I know that hygiene isn’t their major focus there. Yes, I know an artist there doesn’t need any discernible amount of talent to start a parlour. And sure, the Chang beers and the hot sun might have been getting to me.

But as I’ve always said: “Bad decisions make the best travel stories”.

Whilst I wasn’t 100% on the specifics of the design of my tattoo, I was certain that I knew who I wanted to do the work. I was training at Tiger Muay Thai in Chalong, Phuket and across the road, all of 15 metres away, was Chalong Ink. I’m pretty sure their location was planned based simply on the close proximity to drunk travelers at a Muay Thai training camp. Everyone was either trying to look tough or they were too drunk to properly make an informed decision. I wanted to look tough… just like my little sister.

I just read that out loud. Maybe I’ll change my answer to “too drunk”.

Note for my mother: I was not drunk. I’m simply trying to make my life sound more exciting than it actually is. Love you. I’ll call you tonight.

I walked in to Chalong Ink and started to prepare myself by getting out of my pants. The guy working there was polite enough to stop me before I made myself too comfortable, and explained to me I needed to make an appointment to discuss what I would like done. I paid my 500 baht booking fee and agreed to return (fully pant-ed) at 6pm that night. Fast forward to 6pm and not surprisingly I was feeling a lot more comfortable about my decision, probably due to the amount of beer I had ingested while I was waiting.

Honestly, by that stage I was feeling a lot more comfortable about everything thanks to our good friends at that magical place, the Chang beer factory. I discussed with Bamboo Man (I’m not making this up, that was his name) what I would like done and where I would like it. He spoke no English so the “translator” (and I use that term in the loosest possible sense) tried to explain to the Thai local that the English speaking man in front of him wanted a saying tattooed on his chest in Latin.

Doesn’t this sound like the start of a bad joke?

Bamboo Man asked to see what I had in mind. I took out my iPad to show him the design as I imagined it. In hindsight, that act probably increased the cost of my tattoo by about 3000 baht. Rookie mistake. Bamboo Man took my iPad and studied the design. He then nodded to a rough looking westerner and said, “Go”.

I was a little confused as to where I was going, but I followed his friend outside and proceeded to make polite conversation whilst he shaved my chest with a pink razor. Suddenly I was wishing I had more beer. I headed back inside to Bamboo Man where he took out a pen and drew the words freehand across my chest. It seems this wasn’t going to be a discussion about what I wanted, rather he was just going to go ahead and do the job and then charge me way more than he would have if I had come in with a design doodled on a dirty napkin. Oh well. As least they weren’t going to charge me for the shaved chest.

After checking that he had spelled everything correctly, I checked that he had spelled everything correctly. Then, just to make sure, I checked that he had spelled everything correctly. No, I’m not in the process of having a stroke, I just want you to know how really paranoid I was about getting my first tattoo from someone who didn’t speak English in a country where my (lack of) travel insurance wouldn’t mean much if I ended up contracting AIDS. Plus, it would just look ridiculous.

I took my place on the cling wrap covered chair and watched Bamboo Man like a hawk while he collected needles, inks, gloves, alcohol rub, etc. It’s strange but I actually felt like the whole operation was “above board” hygiene-wise. After being offered and rejecting the muscle relaxants on offer, I was ready for Bamboo Man to do his thing.  HOLY SHIT THIS IS THE MOST PAIN I’VE BEEN IN SINCE…

I’m kidding. The chest surprisingly doesn’t hurt all that much. It may have been the 642 Changs I had consumed that day. Or it may have been the muscle relaxants I decided to take after the first couple of minutes. Whatever it was, I felt great!

After three hours of being repeatedly stabbed with several needles attached to a stick, my tattoo was finally complete. I was eager to see it, and my shaved chest, in all its glory. I stood, and slowly turned to face the mirror. That’s when I saw it…  There, on my chest…  Where I expected to see the words “de mortuis nil nisi bonum”…  Was…  Just that.

It seems Bamboo Man had in fact earned his title as 2nd best tattoo artist in all of Thailand for a very good reason. His artwork was faultless. His hygiene standards were impeccable. His muscle relaxants were free. AND I got a free chest shave. It seemed waiting a decade for my first tattoo was worth it.

But be warned; never show your tattoo artist you can afford an iPad.
